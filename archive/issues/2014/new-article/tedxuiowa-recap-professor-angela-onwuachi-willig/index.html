TEDxUIowa Recap: Professor Angela Onwuachi-Willig

Written byCaitlin Scott

You’d be hard-pressed to find a person who isn’t inspired by at least oneTED talk. (Not a TED fan yet? Watch Candy Chang’s popular TED talk and try not cry all your tears. Go on, I dare you).

This year, the UI Center for Student Involvement and Leadership got the TED bug and organized the independent Ted Talk TEDxUIowa. The event took place on Oct. 12 and featured eight accomplished speakers, each with a powerful story to tell relating to TEDxUIowa’s core theme of “excellence.” Three of the eight TEDxUIowa speakers had University of Iowa ties: UI Professor Angela Onwuachi-Willig, UI alum Andy Stoll, and current UI senior Anthony Ferguson. Check out the following recap of Onwuachi-Willig’s captivating story and then get to work on YOUR idea worth spreading.

Angela Onwuachi-Willig: JD, Charles and Marion Kierscht, Professor of Law at the University of Iowa

Angela Onwuachi-Willig at TEDxUiowa. Photo by Mark Zhu / Student Life Marketing + Design

Professor Onwuachi-Willig joined the Iowa Law faculty in 2006 and is the recipient of a number of prestigious awards. Most recently, she won the Marion Huit Award, a University award given to a tenured faculty member in recognition of outstanding teaching, exceptional research and writing, and service to the UI community.She dedicated her speech to a young African-American woman that recently inspired her: Olympic gold medalist Gabby Douglas. Douglas made history at the 2012 London Olympics when she became the first African-American gymnast win gold in the individual all-around competition. Professor Onwuachi-Willig used her 15 minutes to advocate for the importance of recognizing an individual’s race when it comes to their achievements.


Gabby Douglas at the 2012 Olympics in London. Photo: Gregory Bull/AP

“What I can’t relate to is this insistence that we ignore her race; that we ignore the history behind her accomplishments. Because ignoring her race, ignoring the history of these accomplishments, actually discounts the whole magnitude of her achievements,” Onwuachi-Willig says.


Gabby Douglas and her Chinese-American coach Ling Chao. Photo: Julie Jacobson/AP

“It’s hard for me not to smile when I think of these two amazing Americans (Douglas and her Chinese-American coach Ling Chao) and their accomplishments. This is what our country is all about. It’s greatness, its diversity, it’s inclusion, and when you take the racial histories out of this picture, you lose some of its meaning and beauty,” Onwuachi-Willig says. “I continue to be amazed by people like Gabby Douglas; those who can see themselves in a position that no one else like them has ever been in—before many of us can even see them in those positions. She is a seer of hope. She’s a visionary.”
