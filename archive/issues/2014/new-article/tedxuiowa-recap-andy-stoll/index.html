TEDxUIowa Recap: Andy Stoll

Written byCaitlin Scott

You’d be hard-pressed to find a person who isn’t inspired by at least oneTED talk. (Not a TED fan yet? Watch Candy Chang’s popular TED talk and try not cry all your tears. Go on, I dare you).

This year, the UI Center for Student Involvement and Leadership got the TED bug and organized the independent Ted Talk TEDxUIowa. The event took place on Oct. 12 and featured eight accomplished speakers, each with a powerful story to tell relating to TEDxUIowa’s core theme of “excellence.” Three of the eight TEDxUIowa speakers havd University of Iowa ties: UI Professor Angela Onwuachi-Willig, UI alum Andy Stoll, and current UI senior Anthony Ferguson. Check out the following recap of Andy Stoll’s captivating story and then get to work on YOUR idea worth spreading.

Andy Stoll: UI Alum, Media Producer and Social Entrepreneur


Andy Stoll. Photo courtesy of Andy Stoll.

UI alum Andy Stoll is the recipient of Hancher Finkbine Medallion—the highest award the university bestows on students. As an undergraduate student, Stoll served as student body president and the executive director of the Bijou Theater. Stoll is one of the founders of the 10,000 Hours Show. Today, Stoll is busy making good ideas come to life as a social entrepreneur and media producer. Stoll co-founded The James Gang, a social entrepreneurial-incubator that was named the Iowa City Press Citizen ‘Person of the Year’, as the individual or group that had the largest impact on the community. Most recently, Stoll co-founded Seed Here Studio, a social good startup working to grow the entrepreneurial community in the Cedar Rapids-Iowa City area. (To see more of Stoll’s accomplishments, check out his website here.)


Andy Stoll at TEDxUiowa. Photo by Mark Zhu / Student Life Marketing + Desgin.

Stoll began his TEDxUIowa talk by educating the audience on famous social entrepreneurs Muhammad Yunus, Scott Harrison, Jacquie Berglund, and Ben Milne.

“This is an example of social entrepreneurs at their best, four of them that I greatly admire and that are doing really interesting work in the world to solve social problems.” Stoll says. “So, the problem that I have with my own presentation standing up here is that when I was in your seat just 10 years ago, I couldn’t make that logical jump. I’m like, ‘I’m standing in this room at college and these guys are talking about venture capital and Ashton Kutcher and Nobel Peace Prizes and millions of dollars and but how do I get from my chair in college to do this stuff?’”

Stoll really got UI students thinking when he turned the tables and asked them to consider a new set of post-grad questions.


Andy Stoll at TEDxUiowa. Photo by Mark Zhu / Student Life Marketing + Desgin.

“[Social Entrepreneur] is not a traditional job, so you have to throw out all of the traditional questions,” Stoll says. “The traditional questions you ask yourself—that I asked myself when I was in your seat as a student—were these two big questions that freak out a lot of people. ‘What do I want to be when I grow up? What am I going to do when I graduate?’ Let’s just get rid of those questions altogether, because I’ve got a better question. If you want to change the world, if you want to do social entrepreneurial work…the real question is what problem do you want to solve in the world? What problem out there just pisses you off? What problem makes you say, ‘Wow that’s frustrating. Why does that even happen?’ That’s how you become a social entrepreneur.”
