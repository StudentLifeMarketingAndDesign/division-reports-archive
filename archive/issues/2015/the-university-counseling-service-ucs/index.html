University Counseling sees service increases, enacts multicultural initiatives

Written byBerry Schreier & Jeremy Kinser

The year has been both busy and exciting at University Counseling Service (UCS) in terms of staff changes, multicultural commitment, and service increases. With mental health as the fastest growing reason why students seek accommodations and the number one reason students take leave from campus, the UCS continues to provide clinical services, outreach, and training to the UI community at increasing rates each year. The UCS is proud to continue providing thoughtful, compassionate, sensitive, and meaningful mental health services in the coming year.

UCS staff changes

The UCS experienced several exciting Staff changes and additions. Dr. Sam Cochran ended his 33-year tenure at the university as director. Dr. Barry A. Schreier stepped into the role of director bringing strong leadership fashioned while serving as director of the University of Connecticut counseling center and more recently as training director at Princeton University. In addition to new leadership, the UCS welcomed two new staff therapists, Dr. Maria Bruno and Simone Young, M.A.

Multicultural initiatives

The UCS continued its staff-wide investment in Multicultural Outreach and Social Justice Work with under-represented groups. Each staff member forged collaborative relationships with campus units and student groups in the service of coalition building and making campus more inclusive and equitable. Read our Reflective Evaluation detailing the work and staff reflections associated with our work in multiculturalism.

Clinical services 

The UCS Clinical Services continues to see annual increases in student utilization rates with this past year increasing by 2% in individual, couples, and group therapy, psychological testing, case management, and consultation services. Individual therapy remains a primary service followed by group.

In continuing to have greater gender balance in those seeking service, UCS saw increases this year in men seeking service:  Women (61%); Men (37%) or Other Gender Identities (2%). Gender imbalance is constant nationally.

The UCS also saw increased in utilization by students who identify as ethnic minorities. Students who identify as African-American/Black, Asian American/Asian, and Hispanic/Latino/a accessed services at higher rates than in previous years. While there have been increasing numbers of first year students, the majority of clients are juniors, seniors, and graduate students. Since beginning the “University Counseling Service at the School of Dentistry,” service utilization among dental students doubled. There were also notable increased usage rates among engineering students.

UCS Outreach

The UCS provided 1,331 hours of Outreach Programming through 313 programs reaching 15,480 participants. Topics include: academic, UCS 101, stress management, diversity issues/concerns, suicide prevention, working with students in distress, and LGBTQ issues.

UCS maintains liaison relationships with numerous campus groups. An outcome from these liaison relationships was the implementation of seven cultural-based focus groups with Afro-American Cultural Center students, All Latinos Moving Ahead, Asian Pacific American Cultural Center students, Heart Workshop, Latino Council members, and the Native American Student Association. These focus groups provided information related to barriers facing students, opinions related to help-seeking/mental health, knowledge and impressions of the UCS, and ways the UCS could better meet student needs. The UCS is reviewing its findings.

UCS Training

In our direct service to the university’s academic mission, the UCS Training Program provided high quality supervision and didactic training to psychologists-in-training. In turn, the UCS benefits from the work and quality services trainees provide.

The UCS provided training for a total of 13 practicum students, six doctoral interns, and three psychologists delivering services for licensure.  The UCS is proud to have its Pre-Doctoral Internship accredited by the American Psychological Association.

Learn more about University Counseling Service at their website.
